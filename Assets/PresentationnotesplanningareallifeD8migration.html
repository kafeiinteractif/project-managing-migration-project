Do we really have to move? (2 slides?)

If you’re using Drupal 6, you need to move. If you’re using Drupal 7 and you’d like to do some rearchitecting, then you don’t have to move, but it may save time and money in the long run to do it now.

Do you have to use migrate? Frankly, you don’t have to migrate. But you probably should (copy and paste can take a *long* time).  

Here are your two options for migration:

  1. Keep the same architecture, or

  2. create a new structure.

You’re likely going to go down this sophisticated route bc your needs are sophisticated.

What is currently supported:

  • D6 -> D8 migrations,
  • CSV -> D8 migrations (see migrate_plus),
  • Incomplete: D7->D8,
  • Incomplete: XML->D8.

Before you migrate (3 slides?)

  • Have a content stakeholder review the migrations.
  • What do they know about Drupal? About migrations? About content types, nodes, taxonomy, blocks, and views? Ideally, the content stakeholder should have at least a rudimentary understanding of all these things. If they don’t, perhaps introduce them to short videos on the subjects. Don’t give them all the videos at the same time, but as the topics come up, refer them.
  • Enumerate the migrations - how many content types, entity types, etc.
  • Is the migration being done as part of a development process? Is the whole site being migrated, or just part of it?
  • Create a spreadsheet mapping out old to new types/fields/etc. Get your client to review and approve the plan.
  • Have a bug tracker or spreadsheet for content stakeholder to review post-migration
  • General spreadsheet tips:
  • Organize your tabs by the NEW content types.
  • Have an “overview” tab, where you keep a quick list of what is going into what.
  • Keep a notes column for yourself and a notes column for your client.
  • Grant access to your bug tracker if the client is very savvy and/or can be trained to comply with your processes.

The “Moving House” Analogy: Six Action Items for Your Next Migration (1 slide)

  1. Mappings - how to do, how to review. Get approved.
  1. Client wants to see what’s coming with and what’s not.[a] Tech wise this means spreadsheet.
  1. Migrate all the simple things, content types and standard CCK fields, users, files, menus, easy things.
  1. This gives the client assurance that things are indeed getting packed up and moved over.
  2. There are migrations that will bring across your content types as is.
  1. Migrate complex things, write custom queries to get missing data, etc. [review]
  1. hook_prepare_row
  2. plugins
  1. Polish the migration - clean up the body text, filter out “known bad” URLs, take care of any “nice to have” items that are easy. [review again]
  1. identify bad HTML, strip it out
  2. regular expressions, simplehtmldom, etc.
  1. Team review - list out any items that are missing and make a plan to address them. [review again]
  1. list of things to test
  1. Final pre-lauch migration. [review = approval]
  1. launch list - this can also sit on your spreadsheet

Apply the Action Items (5 slides)

Action Item 1 - Mappings - how to do, how to review. Get approved.

  1. Where will things go in the new house?
  1. Create the new content types/new architecture. Here you can decide how best to get your legacy data into customized structures.
  1. Funnel multiple content types into one (one migration for each source)
  2. Break out one legacy content type into multiple new content types (this can be especially helpful if you are improving admin interface or a portal website.
  3. This is also where you can use tools like entity bundles and IEF to make “meta” content types.
  1. Plot it out
  1. Plot the new content types on the spreadsheet. I recommend putting it on the spreadsheet according to the NEW content types.
  2. Plot out new taxonomy and/or menu structures
  1. Formulate your migration templates to provide for this new structure
  1. Deploy your code to the migrate environment.
  1. Expect to have to destroy and rebuild the migrated site.
  2. Use CMI to protect the new house you just built!

Action Items 2 & 3 - Do the actual migrating (first simple, then complex/custom things)

  1. The moving van - get the right vehicle, and keep it tidy
  1. Consider your workflows carefully. All or part of your migration project may benefit from non-standard treatment (ie, not just using migrate).  Keep an open mind when you are planning your migration.
  1. Drupal Migrate
  2. CSV, XML (soon!) imports
  3. Copy & Paste
  1. In D7 you could use the Migrate UI to help reviewing code. This doesn’t exist (yet) in D8, so maybe use Drush with migrate_plus to see status.
  2. DNM - keep yourself organized by putting DNM sections at the top of each migration.

Action Items 4 & 5 - Internal Testing and cleanup

  1. Unpack and put away [XX use list of multiple review points above]
  1. Create a list of things for testing.  Standard testing stuff also includes
  1. tags in body text
  2. Stubs
  1. When you ask people to test, make sure to include a representative node, so that people know what they’re looking at/for. Otherwise, they won’t do it. They really won’t.
  2. Create a bug/issue master list spreadsheet (or use a bug tracker like Jira)
  1. Give client and dev their own columns
  2. Ask people to put their initials before their comments
  1. As you polish the migrations, there’s a tendency to leave documentation and organization behind. Keep it clean and documented!

Action Item 6 - External Testing, Launch, and Post Launch

  1. Housewarming!
  1. Take your time when showing clients how to navigate the new site.
  2. Ask them to collaborate on help text - for more advanced/trusted users, have them modify the help text.
  3. “Give” the Test site to the client to make changes on.
  1. Here’s where it helps to have more than one site (having a Dev, a Test, and a Live site is usually best practice)
  2. Make sure that client knows that this is a turn and burn process - D8 has no updates, so it will be a complete rollback (ie, fresh site) every time.
  1. Say goodbye to the old house
  1. Deprecate the site gracefully. If possible, make the old site available (at least to admin users) at a subdomain such as old.example.com.  Keep a copy of the legacy database and the migration code safe and accessible.
  2. When all is said and done, there will be some dust bunnies left at the old home. Document them as best you can and move on.

Tools for developers (1 slide)

  • Drupal’s core migrate and migrate_drupal modules

  • Contrib migrate_ui (all-in-one), migrate_plus (CSV)

Process Advice (1 slide)

  • An important part of migrations are keeping things orderly, documenting your process, using clear and consistent naming conventions.  This also means that you’ll need to keep conscious of legacy data (whether or not it comes with you, it should be purposeful), and try to structure the new site to be a good archive.  For example, are there content types that have been doing too many things?  Are there things in the WYSIWYG that should be pulled out and made into content types?

Talking Points (not for slides)

  • Taxonomy
  • How does the taxonomy work for their old site?
  • What kinds of changes are they expecting?
  • Do they have a taxonomist (for many cases, it’s actually recommendable!)
  • Is their site content type driven or taxonomy driven?
  • Are their taxonomies heavily integrated with their menu system?
  • Content modeling
  • This isn’t the most fun, and hopefully you won’t have to do it.  But realistically, you may be handed a project where the content and presentation modelling falls to you.
  • Read a bit about it: http://www.weaveweb.com.au/blog/content-modelling-what-why-and-how
  • Think about it - a lot!  Make spreadsheets and diagrams and mind-maps, and whatever else will help you wrap your head around what the legacy site is AND what the the new site will (hopefully) be like.
  • Read the content, at least some of it.
